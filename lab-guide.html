Section 1

The Greenhouse Effect

Earth is warm enough for liquid water (and for us!) because of a thin blanket of gases in the atmosphere. Sunlight, mostly visible light, but also ultraviolet (UV), and infrared (IR), passes through the atmosphere and warms the surface. The warmed surface radiates that energy back out as infrared (heat) radiation. Certain gases absorb that outgoing infrared and re-emit it in all directions, including back down. The result: the surface stays warmer than it would if that energy escaped straight to space.

Without any greenhouse gases, Earth's average surface temperature would be roughly −18 °C (about 0 °F) resulting in a frozen planet. With them, it's about +15 °C. That ~33 °C of warming is the natural greenhouse effect, and it is a good thing. The concern with climate change is not the existence of the effect — it's that we are strengthening it.

Section 2

The Carbon Cycle

Carbon is constantly moving between four great reservoirs: the atmosphere, the oceans, the land (soils, plants, rock), and fossil deposits (coal, oil, gas). The movement of carbon between these is the carbon cycle. For most of Earth's history these flows were roughly balanced, carbon released by volcanoes and respiration was taken up by photosynthesis, weathering, and the oceans.

The crucial distinction is between the fast cycle and the slow cycle. The fast cycle moves carbon between air, life, and surface ocean over years to centuries. The slow cycle locks carbon into rock and fossil fuels over millions of years. Burning fossil fuels takes carbon the slow cycle buried over tens of millions of years and returns it to the atmosphere in a couple of centuries, far faster than the natural sinks can absorb it.

Section 3

Reading the Climate of the Past

Thermometers have only existed for a few centuries, and direct CO₂ measurements began in 1958. To know climate before that, scientists use proxies, natural recorders that preserve a signal of past conditions. The headline proxy, and the anchor of this lab, is the ice core.

Ice cores: bubbles of ancient air

In Antarctica and Greenland, snow falls, compacts, and never melts. Each year's snow traps tiny bubbles of the atmosphere as it turns to ice. Drill down through that ice and you drill back through time, the deepest Antarctic cores reach about 1.7 miles or 1,200,000 years. Crack open a bubble and you can measure the actual ancient air: a direct sample of past CO₂. Meanwhile, the ratio of heavy to light water isotopes in the ice itself records the temperature when that snow fell. One core gives you both CO₂ and temperature, layer by layer.

Ice cores

Trapped air bubbles give direct CO₂; water isotopes give temperature back ~1,200,000 years.

Tree rings

Width and density of annual growth rings record temperature and moisture back a few thousand years.

Ocean sediment

Shells of tiny marine organisms (foraminifera) lock in isotopes recording ocean temperature back millions of years.

Corals & speleothems

Growth bands in coral and cave formations record temperature, rainfall, and ocean chemistry.

Why this matters: Because ice cores give us real ancient air, we are not guessing about past CO₂, we are measuring it. That's what makes the comparison between past and present so powerful, and it's exactly what you'll do in the activity.

Section 4

Natural Cycles vs. the Modern Change

Earth's climate has always changed. Over the last million years it has swung between cold glacial periods (ice ages) and warm interglacial periods like the one we live in now. These swings are paced by Milankovitch cycles — slow, predictable wobbles in Earth's orbit and tilt that change how sunlight is distributed. Each full glacial–interglacial cycle takes roughly 100,000 years.

Crucially, CO₂ rides along with these cycles. During glacials, ice-core CO₂ drops to around 180-200 ppm; during warm interglacials it rises to about 280 ppm. That ~100 ppm swing took thousands of years each time and never broke past ~300 ppm for 800,000 years straight.

Today, atmospheric CO₂ is over 430 ppm, far above anything in that entire 800,000-year record, and it climbed there in about 150 years, not thousands. The natural cycles are real, and the modern change is genuinely different: not in that it's happening, but in its rate and its cause.

Optional · Not Graded

Deeper Time: CO₂ and Mass Extinctions

The 800,000-year ice-core record is the high-resolution window. But CO₂ has shaped climate across hundreds of millions of years too. Over deep time, atmospheric CO₂ has ranged far wider than the ice-core window, sometimes many times today's level. Several of Earth's five great mass extinctions are linked to rapid changes in CO₂ and climate.

The clearest example is the end-Permian extinction (~252 million years ago), the largest in Earth's history, which wiped out an estimated 90% of marine species. A leading explanation involves massive volcanism (the Siberian Traps) injecting enormous quantities of CO₂ into the atmosphere, driving rapid warming and ocean acidification. The pattern, a fast carbon pulse causing fast warming, is part of why scientists watch today's rapid CO₂ rise with concern.

This section is optional and not graded. It's here for context if you're curious how the CO₂-climate link plays out over the longest timescales. The graded activity focuses on the ice-core and modern records.
